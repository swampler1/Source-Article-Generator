Overview

V* MY Lup is a young low-mass stellar object located in the Lupus star-forming region, characterized by its protoplanetary disk. This disk exhibits complex structures and dynamics, making MY Lup a significant target for studies of accretion processes and planet formation. The object is cataloged under various identifiers, including TIC 255464386, Gaia DR3 5995177933191206016, and others. The study of MY Lup has revealed important insights into the physical and chemical properties of protoplanetary disks, as well as the accretion mechanisms at play in young stellar objects.

Disk Structure and Kinematics

The protoplanetary disk surrounding MY Lup has been observed to have a high inclination, with a disk inclination of approximately 70 degrees and a position angle (PA) of 140 degrees, as derived from ALMA observations [DECO table]. The disk exhibits a complex structure, including rings and gaps, indicative of ongoing dynamical processes. Observations from ALMA have revealed dark bands and bright rings at specific distances, with the presence of CO2 and HCN isotopologues providing insights into the disk's temperature and column density distributions [2]. The disk's physical structure is further characterized by a distance of approximately 150 parsecs from Earth, a stellar mass of about 0.7 solar masses, and a systemic velocity of 6.5 km/s [DECO table].

The kinematics of the disk have been studied using high-resolution CO observations, which indicate a vertical distribution of CO emission that varies with radius. The emission surfaces trace approximately 2-5 times the gas pressure scale heights, suggesting a flared disk structure that influences the dynamics of gas and dust within the disk [6]. The analysis of CO emission has also revealed local temperature variations that correspond to dust substructures, providing insights into the potential locations of embedded planets [6]. 

The presence of annular substructures in the disk has been linked to planet-disk interactions, with the morphology of these features suggesting that they may play a role in the accretion processes occurring within the disk [9]. The study of these structures is crucial for understanding the mechanisms of planet formation and the evolution of protoplanetary disks.

Outflows and Jets

MY Lup has been classified as a transitional disk candidate, which implies the presence of complex accretion environments and potential outflows. Observations from the Hubble Space Telescope have indicated active accretion processes, with a revised mass accretion rate (˙Macc) estimated to be approximately 1(+1.5, -0.5) x 10^(-8) M⊙/yr [1]. This accretion rate is consistent with typical values for young stellar objects in the Lupus region, although discrepancies exist between different observational methods. The high inclination of the disk suggests that the geometry may influence the observed accretion indicators, complicating the interpretation of outflow dynamics.

The study of outflows in MY Lup has been further enhanced by the use of high-resolution spectroscopy from the James Webb Space Telescope (JWST). Observations have revealed the presence of molecular species such as HCN and CO, which are crucial for understanding the chemical pathways involved in outflow formation [7]. The relationship between the observed outflows and the disk's accretion processes remains an area of active research, with ongoing studies aiming to reconcile the various indicators of accretion and outflow dynamics.

Physical Conditions

The physical conditions within the MY Lup disk are characterized by a range of temperatures and densities, influenced by the disk's structure and the presence of molecular species. The MIRI-MRS spectrum of MY Lup has revealed emissions from various elements, including CO2, HCN, and water vapor, with a signal-to-noise ratio of approximately 300 [2]. The analysis of these emissions has provided insights into the isotopic compositions and molecular properties within the disk.

Temperature estimates derived from the spectral data indicate a range of approximately 170-220 K in the inner disk regions, suggesting a radially-extended reservoir of material near the snowline [5]. The complexity of modeling CO2 emissions has been highlighted due to blending and degeneracies between parameters, necessitating detailed analysis to derive accurate temperature and column density estimates [2]. The presence of water vapor and other molecular species in the disk is critical for understanding the chemical processes that govern planet formation and the evolution of protoplanetary disks.

Observational Methods

The study of MY Lup has employed a variety of observational methods, utilizing both ground-based and space-based telescopes to gather comprehensive data on the disk's properties. The Hubble Space Telescope has been instrumental in measuring the mass accretion rate through observations of emission lines and far-ultraviolet continuum excess emission [1]. The JWST has provided high-resolution mid-infrared spectroscopy, allowing for the detection of various molecular species and isotopologues within the disk [2, 5].

ALMA has played a crucial role in imaging the disk's structure at millimeter wavelengths, revealing the presence of rings and gaps that indicate complex dynamical processes [6, 9]. The combination of these observational techniques has enabled researchers to derive key parameters such as disk inclination, position angle, and systemic velocity, which are essential for understanding the disk's dynamics and evolution [DECO table].

The analysis of the data has involved sophisticated modeling techniques, including slab models for spectral fitting and iterative procedures for refining surface density profiles [2, 8]. The integration of multi-wavelength observations has proven vital for constructing a comprehensive picture of the physical and chemical conditions within the MY Lup disk.

Key Results and Interpretation

The research surrounding MY Lup has yielded several key results that enhance our understanding of protoplanetary disks and the processes of planet formation. The estimated mass accretion rate of approximately 1(+1.5, -0.5) x 10^(-8) M⊙/yr indicates active accretion processes, although discrepancies between different observational methods highlight the complexities involved in measuring accretion rates in transitional disks [1]. The presence of rings and gaps in the disk, as observed by ALMA, suggests ongoing interactions between the disk material and potential forming planets, which may influence the disk's evolution and the formation of planetary systems [9].

The detection of various molecular species, including CO2, HCN, and water vapor, provides insights into the chemical composition of the disk and the conditions under which planets may form [2, 5]. The temperature estimates derived from these observations indicate a dynamic environment that is conducive to the formation of solid bodies, with implications for understanding the origins of volatile elements in planetary systems.

The ongoing studies of MY Lup emphasize the importance of high-resolution observations and multi-wavelength approaches in unraveling the complexities of protoplanetary disks. The findings contribute to a broader understanding of the mechanisms driving accretion, outflow dynamics, and the chemical evolution of disks, ultimately informing theories of planet formation and the development of planetary systems.
